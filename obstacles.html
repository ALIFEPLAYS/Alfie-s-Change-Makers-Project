Obstacles

Difficulties with gathering Data

Some difficulties may be associated with the gathering of accurate data for the labels. Some of the major barriers are as follows: difficulty in measuring the pollutants being produced and where they are ending up; gathering data from companies earlier in the supply chain, which may be in different countries, may be difficult to verify; and gathering the data may apply extra costs to companies and be expensive to accurately verify.

1. Although general mass balance calculations can be made regarding the production of pollutants, these may not be completely accurate, as some changes during production may occur. It may also be wrong to assume that all pollutants would have the same negative effect if they are present in the same quantities; the location where they are released may affect the severity of the pollutant.

2. Countries have different regulations related to the way waste is monitored. In addition, countries with weaker economies may find it difficult to obtain sufficient capital to meet the standards we have set out.

3. Some of the costs required for accurate monitoring can include the installation of monitoring equipment, laboratory studies, environmental consultancy, and third-party verification. These costs could accumulate and make it unfeasible for the company to sell its products in the UK at their current prices. Depending on the market, this could lead to prices increasing for consumers or the company withdrawing from the market. This is even more impactful on upstream companies in weaker economies, as the capital required and access to verifiers are more difficult to obtain. This could lead them to stop supplying upstream companies.

Possible market affects of the labels introduction

Due to the increase in supply costs across the entire supply chain, costs at all levels may rise significantly. This may have drastic consequences, as many of the products being produced are essential household goods. Because the demand for necessities has very little room to decrease, higher production costs may translate directly into higher retail prices and/or supply shortages, which could be highly inflationary for the UK economy.

Another possible effect of increased costs is that downstream companies may stop sourcing from certain upstream suppliers if the costs of compliance become too high. This could lead upstream companies to seek alternative suppliers that are either more expensive or less environmentally friendly. As a result, the intended environmental benefits of the system could be partially undermined if companies prioritise cost reductions over environmental performance.

Even in cases where price rises are not extremely large, problems may still arise following the establishment of environmental ratings. Products with stronger environmental performance may begin to increase in price relative to competing products, particularly if their production processes require more careful monitoring or cleaner technologies. This change in relative prices may lead some consumers, particularly those who derive less personal utility from environmentally friendly consumption, to choose the lower-rated but cheaper option.

In addition, consumers who do wish to make environmentally responsible choices may be priced out of the more environmentally friendly alternatives. This could create a situation where sustainable consumption becomes disproportionately accessible to higher-income households, potentially limiting the overall environmental impact of the labelling system and raising concerns about fairness and accessibility.

Ways to solve these problems

Some potential solutions to these problems may include several adjustments to the way environmental data is reported and verified.

1. Giving a rating to the quality of the data

Pros: This would allow companies some flexibility regarding the accuracy of the data they provide, potentially reducing the cost pressures associated with highly precise monitoring and verification. A data quality rating could also give consumers transparency about how reliable the underlying measurements are.

Cons: Companies may be able to manipulate datasets or rely on lower-quality estimates, which could make other companies appear worse in comparison if their estimated values are lower than the true values. Another drawback is that introducing a data quality indicator may make the label more complex, potentially reducing consumer understanding and decreasing the amount of attention paid to it at the point of purchase.

2. Including an uncertainty range for the value

Pros: Presenting results with an uncertainty range would allow for less precise measurements without significantly affecting the overall interpretation of the data. This approach is commonly used in scientific reporting and could provide a more honest representation of the limitations of environmental measurements.

Cons: Adding uncertainty ranges may create additional visual clutter on the label. If the information becomes too detailed or technical, consumers may find the label harder to interpret quickly while shopping.

3. Having different standards of verification depending on the location of the company in the supply chain

Pros: This approach would maintain high standards of accuracy where monitoring is easier and more affordable such as at later stages of the supply chain, while giving some flexibility to companies facing larger financial or logistical barriers. This could help ensure broader participation in the labelling system.

Cons: A potential risk is that companies may deliberately switch to suppliers located in regions with weaker monitoring or verification requirements. This could reduce the overall reliability of the system and potentially undermine the environmental goals of the labelling scheme.
